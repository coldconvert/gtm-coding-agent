# Chapter 09: Voice DNA & Content

**AI-generated content has a smell. Your prospects can detect it. Voice DNA is how you extract your real writing voice into a structured profile so coding agents produce content that sounds like you, not like a robot.**

---

## The Slop Problem

You've seen it. We've all seen it. LinkedIn posts that start with "In today's rapidly evolving landscape..." Blog posts packed with "leverage," "synergy," and "game-changer." Emails that read like they were generated by feeding a job title into a template.

This is slop. And slop is the new spam.

Here's why it matters for GTM: your buyers read 50-100 emails a week. They scroll past hundreds of LinkedIn posts. Their filter for "this was written by a human who actually thinks about this stuff" vs "this was generated by AI and blasted to 10,000 people" is razor-sharp and getting sharper.

AI-generated content isn't bad because it's AI-generated. It's bad because most people use AI with zero context about how they actually write. So the output defaults to generic corporate voice. The median of everything Claude has ever read.

You don't sound like the median. Nobody does. Voice DNA fixes this.

---

## What Voice DNA Is

Voice DNA is a structured profile that captures how you write. Not how you want to write. Not how you think a "thought leader" should sound. How you actually sound when you're writing well.

It includes:
- **Identity** — who you are, what you do, what you've earned the right to talk about
- **Tone markers** — are you dry? blunt? self-deprecating? technical? informal?
- **Vocabulary patterns** — words you reach for naturally, phrases that recur in your writing
- **Sentence structure** — do you write short punchy sentences? Long flowing ones? A mix?
- **Anti-patterns** — words and phrases you never use, because they don't sound like you
- **Platform rules** — how your voice adapts across LinkedIn, email, blog, and other channels

When this profile lives in your workspace as a structured markdown file, a coding agent can reference it every time it writes content for you. The output goes from "generic AI" to "sounds like me on a good day."

## How to Extract Your Voice

This is a practical exercise. Do it now or bookmark it, but don't skip it if you create content as part of your GTM.

### Step 1: Gather your samples

Find 5-10 pieces of your best writing. Not your most polished — your most *you*. Places to look:

- LinkedIn posts that got real engagement (not just impressions — actual comments from people who got something out of it)
- Emails you sent to prospects or customers that got replies
- Blog posts or newsletter issues you're proud of
- Slack messages or internal docs where you explained something well
- Twitter/X threads if that's your platform

Copy the raw text. Don't edit it. The agent needs to see your natural voice, not a cleaned-up version.

### Step 2: Feed them to Claude Code

Open Claude Code in your GTM workspace and give it the samples along with this prompt:

```
I'm going to paste 5-10 writing samples. These are my actual voice — LinkedIn posts,
emails, and blog content I've written.

Analyze these samples and create a voice DNA profile with:

1. **Identity**: who I am based on what I write about and how I write
2. **Tone markers**: 5-7 adjectives that describe my writing voice
3. **Vocabulary patterns**: words and phrases I use often, natural to my style
4. **Sentence structure**: short/long/mixed, paragraph length, use of lists
5. **What I never say**: words, phrases, and patterns absent from my writing
6. **Platform differences**: any differences between my LinkedIn vs email vs blog voice

Be specific. Don't give me generic advice. Ground everything in the actual samples.
```

Then paste your samples.

### Step 3: Review and edit

The agent will produce a voice profile. Read it critically. It should make you say "yeah, that's how I write" at least 80% of the time. Edit the parts that feel off. Add things it missed. Remove anything that doesn't ring true.

### Step 4: Save it to your workspace

Save the profile to `templates/voice/core-voice.md` in your GTM workspace. This becomes a reference file that your CLAUDE.md can point to whenever content gets created.

## The Voice Profile Structure

Here's what your finished voice profile should look like. This maps to the templates in this repo at `templates/voice/`.

### `templates/voice/core-voice.md`

Your identity, tone, vocabulary, and structure. The master file an agent reads before writing anything.

```markdown
# Voice DNA — [Your Name]

## Identity
- Role: founder / GTM engineer / agency operator
- Domain: what you sell, who you sell to
- Authority: what gives you the right to write about this

## Tone
- [5-7 tone markers, e.g.: direct, technical, occasionally dry humor]
- Default register: [casual / professional-casual / formal]

## Vocabulary
- Words I use naturally: [list]
- Phrases I reach for: [list]
- Technical terms I use without explaining: [list]

## Structure
- Sentence length: [short / mixed / long]
- Paragraph length: [1-2 sentences / 3-4 sentences]
- Lists: [frequent / occasional / rare]
- Questions to the reader: [yes, often / sometimes / never]

## What I Sound Like
[Paste 2-3 sentences from your best writing that capture your voice perfectly]
```

### `templates/voice/anti-slop.md`

The kill list. Words and patterns that are banned from any content generated in your workspace.

### `templates/voice/platform-playbook.md`

How your voice adapts per channel. LinkedIn rhythm is different from email is different from blog.

## The Anti-Slop Rules

This is the highest-ROI section in this chapter. These rules go in your `anti-slop.md` file and get referenced in your CLAUDE.md. When an agent sees these rules, it avoids the patterns that make AI content detectable.

### Words to ban

| Instead of | Write |
|---|---|
| leverage | use |
| utilize | use |
| streamline | speed up, simplify |
| game-changer | [be specific: "cut our enrichment time from 2 hours to 4 minutes"] |
| cutting-edge | new, latest |
| revolutionary | [just describe what it does] |
| synergy | [delete the sentence] |
| elevate | improve, raise |
| robust | solid, strong |
| unlock | get, access, enable |
| deep dive | look at, dig into |

### Patterns to ban

- "In today's [anything]..." — delete the whole sentence. Start with the point.
- "Let me be clear:" — just be clear. You don't need to announce it.
- "Here's the thing:" — used sparingly and only if it's actually your voice. Most people don't talk like this.
- Opening with a question you immediately answer — "What if I told you..." No.
- Three em dashes in one paragraph — unless that's genuinely how you write.
- Ending every section with a one-sentence power line — this is the most detectable AI pattern on LinkedIn right now.

### The test

Read your generated content out loud. If you wouldn't say it to a colleague over coffee, rewrite it. That's the whole test.

## Platform Adaptation

Your voice stays the same. The format changes.

### LinkedIn

- Short paragraphs. 1-2 sentences max per block.
- White space is your friend. The feed is dense — your post needs to breathe.
- Hook in the first line. Not clickbait — a real hook that earns the next line.
- No links in the post body. LinkedIn's algorithm buries posts with outbound links. Put links in the first comment.
- Personal experience beats abstract advice. "I did X and learned Y" outperforms "5 tips for Z" every time.

### Email (outbound)

- One ask per email. Not two. Not "and also." One.
- Keep it under 100 words for cold outbound. Under 150 for warm.
- Sound like a person, not a company. First person singular. "I" not "we."
- Reference something specific about them. Not "I saw your company is growing" — that's generic. "I saw you hired 3 AEs in the last month" — that's a signal.

### Blog / long-form

- More room to be detailed and technical.
- Subheads matter. Readers scan first, read second.
- Include real numbers, real examples, real screenshots where possible.
- Your voice can relax here. Blog readers chose to click — you don't need to earn every line the way you do on LinkedIn.

## Why This Matters for GTM

Two reasons.

**Outbound:** Personalized emails that sound like a real human get 3-5x more replies than templated slop. When your coding agent writes outbound using your voice profile + your ICP + your positioning, the output is a personalized email that reads like you sat down and wrote it for that specific person. Because structurally, that's what happened — the agent just did the sitting down part.

**Content:** Consistent voice builds trust. Trust builds pipeline. When someone reads your LinkedIn post, clicks your profile, reads three more posts, and they all sound like the same person with the same perspective — that's brand equity you can't buy. Voice DNA is what makes that possible at scale without everything blurring into generic AI output.

## Key Takeaways

- AI content sounds like AI because it has no context about your voice. Voice DNA fixes that.
- Extract your voice from real writing samples, not from how you think you should sound.
- Anti-slop rules are the highest-leverage thing you can add to your workspace. Ten banned words will improve every piece of content.
- Your voice stays the same across platforms. The format is what changes.
- This isn't vanity work. Voice consistency directly drives reply rates and pipeline.
